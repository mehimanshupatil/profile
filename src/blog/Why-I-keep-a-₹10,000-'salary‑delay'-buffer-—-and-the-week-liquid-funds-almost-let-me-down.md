It was a Monday, payroll delayed, and my landlord’s WhatsApp had a blue bubble that read: “Please confirm rent.” I had ₹50 in my wallet, exactly enough for two cups of chai. Everything else — my cushion, the "emergency fund" I felt smug about — was parked where it earned a few basis points more than a savings account: a liquid mutual fund at ₹10,000.

I'd made the move because savings accounts feel lazy in 2024: 3–4% interest, zero discipline. Liquid funds give a better return, auto‑redeem works, and payments can hit my bank within a business day. It seemed like a small upgrade with no real tradeoff. That week taught me otherwise.

Why I moved my emergency stash out of a savings account
I moved because laziness with money has a cost. My savings account sat at ~₹15,000, and every year it shrank relative to inflation. I wanted my buffer to earn something, not sit like a dead weight. Liquid funds felt safe: high credit quality, same‑day NAV often, and a better yield (₹10,000 at ~5–6% annualized felt better than 3%).

Practical reasons that made sense for me:
- It was easy to redeem from Zerodha/Paytm Money/ET Money — click, money in bank.
- I didn't want temptation. Automatic sweep‑in fixed deposits felt like overkill; liquid funds were a compromise.
- I track finances in one app and hated multiple logins. Consolidation wins.

The weekend my plan hit a wall
Payroll ran late that month. Not uncommon in Indian startups — approvals stuck, payroll vendor glitches. I logged into my fund app on Monday morning, hit redeem, and saw a message I hadn't noticed before: "Redemption T+1 for settlements — funds transferred within 24 hours to registered bank account."

I assumed it would be the usual few hours. It took 30. But my bank used a non‑instant settlement path because the redemption had to route through the AMC, registrar, then my bank. Add to that a holiday in the clearing cycle for my bank, and the transfer slipped into the next working day.

Day one: polite messages to my landlord, a promise that rent would be paid by Tuesday.

Day two: my fund redemptions reached the bank but were marked as 'NEFT processing' because my bank's inward system wasn't real‑time that morning. My landlord's patience ran out. He wanted the money that afternoon.

I ended up withdrawing the ₹5,000 cash from a friend who overnighted it to me and paid him back via UPI once the NEFT landed in my account. I also learned that UPI refunds and other money flows can take unpredictable paths; my friend's transfer was instant, mine wasn't.

What I missed planning for
- Settlement timings: Liquid funds settle to bank via registrar (KFin/CAMS) on T+1 and sometimes show up only after NEFT/IMPS processing.
- Bank quirks: Not all banks post inward transfers at the same time of day; some process NEFT batches late.
- Human timelines: Landlords, office admin, or clinics rarely care about your T+1. They want the money now.
- Edge cases: Refunds from platforms (IRCTC, airlines) and bounced UPI transactions sometimes take longer and don't respect 'instant' labels.

The honest failure: I underestimated "immediate"
I made a spreadsheet of my cash flows, emergency buffer, and a couple of 'what ifs'. But I treated "liquid" as equivalent to "instant." That's my failure. In practice, 'instant' is a combination of the fund house's processing, the bank's timings, and luck.

This wasn't a catastrophic loss. I didn't miss rent for long and I repaid my friend the next day. But the experience changed the psychological value of the ₹10,000 fund. An "emergency fund" that needs a day to become actionable isn't an emergency fund for sudden cash needs.

How I adjusted the system (what I actually changed)
I didn't undo the move — I refined it.

1) I split the money based on "urgency of access"
- ₹2,500 cash envelope at home for day‑to‑day, last‑mile needs (auto‑refill every two weeks).
- ₹7,500 in a liquid fund for short‑term buffer and higher yield. I treat this as my primary cushion, but accept T+1 constraints.
Why ₹2,500? It covers rent advances, a couple of taxi rides home, or a lunch + groceries until redemptions clear. Small, boring, but immediate.

2) I use a fast bank connection for critical inflows
- My primary salary account is with a bank that posts inbound transfers faster for my fund redemptions (I moved that deliberately after the incident).
- For friend advances I keep a prepaid card with ₹1,000 loaded — cheap insurance.

3) I changed communication defaults
- I tell my landlord or office admin: "Rent by 3rd day is fine unless you need it earlier" and keep a small buffer specifically for rent cycles (₹7,500 in the liquid fund + ₹2,500 cash).
- This is awkward but honest — and in my experience, people prefer clarity over excuses.

4) I set a rule for "immediate vs short notice"
- Immediate: anything that must be paid within hours — use cash or instant bank balance.
- Short notice: payments due within 48 hours — redeem liquid fund.
This reduces my panic and avoids emergency borrowing.

Tradeoffs and irritation
Keeping cash is old‑school. I dislike it. It’s petty to store ₹2,500 at home when UPI is everywhere. But the psychological relief is real. Also, the ₹7,500 in liquid funds will underperform a more diversified emergency stash (sweep FD, overnight funds), but it’s convenient and low friction. There’s a tiny yield tradeoff to keep immediate liquidity.

Takeaway (what I actually walked away with)
Liquidity has layers. Liquid mutual funds are better than idle savings for a buffer — as long as you accept T+1 and bank quirks. For true "emergencies" that need cash within hours, keep a token of immediate liquidity (cash or an account that guarantees instant credits). For me, that was a ₹2,500 cash envelope plus ₹7,500 in liquid funds. It’s small, ugly, and practical — and it stopped me borrowing from friends at odd hours.

A question I still live with: how much of your emergency fund should live in instant pipes vs slightly slower, higher‑yield places? My answer is conservative and local: a little instant cash goes a long way in India.
